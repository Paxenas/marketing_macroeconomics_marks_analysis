Section 1

Marketing Topics — Ranked

Ranked by combined score: frequency across both exams × marks allocated × teacher emphasis. Frequency dots = how often seen across 2 exams.

1
Pricing — All Methods & Calculations
Cost-based pricing Demand-based Competition-based Price differentiation Group pricing Product versioning
Teacher focus: Calculation steps must be shown fully. Correct cost allocation per unit (fixed/variable split). Terminology is rewarded — use "selling price", "total cost", "profit margin" precisely. Demand/competition pricing needs a real-world application example.
CRITICAL ≈ 24 pts
2
Contribution Margin & Break-Even Analysis
CM I per unit & total CM II (after fixed costs) Break-even quantity formula Break-even graph (3 curves) Fixed vs. variable cost classification
Teacher focus: The graph must show three distinct curves: variable costs, total costs, and revenue. Break-even point must be highlighted explicitly. CM I = price − variable cost per unit; CM II = CM I (total) − fixed costs. Misclassifying fixed vs. variable costs (e.g. fuel) was penalized.
CRITICAL ≈ 22 pts
3
Price Elasticity
Demand curve (graph) Elasticity formula & calculation Elastic vs. inelastic interpretation Cross-price elasticity Income elasticity Turnover at given price
Teacher focus: Formula must be shown explicitly before substituting values. Axes must be correctly labelled (price on x-axis, demand on y-axis in this context). PE > 1 = elastic; analysis sentence is required. Cross-price and income elasticity needed a real-world sentence example.
CRITICAL ≈ 16 pts
4
BCG Matrix (Growth-Share Matrix)
Draw & label matrix Sort products (relative market share calc) Purpose of each quadrant Cash cows → fund stars/question marks
Teacher focus: Relative market share = company turnover ÷ strongest competitor. Products must be placed correctly; students lost marks for wrong quadrant placement (B incorrectly placed in KA 1). Must explain the purpose of cash cows clearly: "generate cash to fund stars and question marks."
HIGH ≈ 12 pts
5
SWOT & PESTLE Analysis
Identify S/W/O/T from a text Internal vs. external distinction Name all 6 PESTLE aspects Label paragraphs to correct PESTLE factor
Teacher focus: SWOT must be taken directly from the given text — vague answers ("competition") were penalized as "too vague." PESTLE labelling was heavily weighted (6 pts). Internal = strengths & weaknesses; External = opportunities & threats.
HIGH ≈ 19 pts
6
Ansoff Matrix & Porter's Generic Strategies
Label 4 Ansoff quadrants Classify real-world examples (1–4) Label Porter's 4 strategies Apply Porter to a company scenario
Teacher focus: All 4 Ansoff and all 4 Porter strategies must be spelled correctly and placed correctly. Classification of examples (e.g. BurgerKing = 4=Diversification) carries 6 pts. Porter application: "Face Protector" sells globally → Cost Leadership (broad + cost).
HIGH ≈ 16 pts
7
Market Research, Segmentation & ATL/BTL
Primary vs. secondary research (explain + adv/dis) 4 segmentation criteria with explanations ATL = mass/Above the Line BTL = targeted/Below the Line
Teacher focus: Each segmentation criterion needs a clear explanation, not just a name. ATL/BTL: examples required (TV ad, influencer marketing). Research advantages/disadvantages must be distinct — "fast & cheap" for secondary; "precise & fresh" for primary.
HIGH ≈ 21 pts
8
4Ps, PLC, Diversification & Brand Marketing
4Ps: name + explain each Marketing definition PLC: draw 5 phases (incl. Development) Product diversification types Multi-branding pros/cons Brand advocates
Teacher focus: PLC must show the Development phase below the x-axis (before launch). Diversification matching requires all 6 terms correctly defined. Brand advocate definition must go beyond "word of mouth" → mention "opinion leaders."
MEDIUM ≈ 30 pts
Section 2

Gesamtwirtschaft — Ranked

Ranked across KA 3 (80 pts) and KA 4 (50 pts). Topics appearing in both exams receive highest priority.

1
Employment — Types, Causes & Policy Responses
Frictional unemployment Cyclical unemployment Structural unemployment Labour market imperfections Government measures Negative impacts on individuals/companies/governments
Teacher focus: All 3 types must be defined accurately with examples. Government measures must match the specific imperfection listed. Negative impacts require separate answers per stakeholder (individual, company, government) — vague combined answers lost marks.
CRITICAL ≈ 20 pts
2
Fiscal Policy — Demand-Sided Anti-Cyclical
Recession measures (tax cuts + govt spending) Multiplier/chain effect explanation Why cheques might not work (savings paradox) Raising taxes in recession — counter-argument Recession spiral: demand → production → employment
Teacher focus: "Suggest" = always explain WHY and HOW the measure works, not just name it. The chain (lower tax → more disposable income → higher demand → more production → more employment) must be stated in full. Cheque scenario: key is people may save instead of spend.
CRITICAL ≈ 15 pts
3
Price Stability — CPI, Inflation & Deflation
CPI measurement (basket of goods) HCPI vs. CPI difference Impact of inflation on purchasing power Demand-pull vs. cost-push inflation 12 True/False statements on inflation/deflation
Teacher focus: The T/F section (12 BE) was the single largest task block in KA 3. Teacher tested nuanced distinctions: "The aim is near-zero inflation (around 2%), NOT zero." Deflation long-run = demand falls (people wait for lower prices). Key word: "usually" in T/F statements changes the answer.
CRITICAL ≈ 20 pts
4
Appropriate Growth — GDP, HDI & True/False
Nominal vs. Real GDP (contrast) Real GDP calculation (deflator method) HDI: 3 pillars (health, education, income) GDP limitations (unpaid work, wellbeing) True/False statements on GDP
Teacher focus: Real GDP = Nominal GDP ÷ Deflator. Deflator = 1 + inflation rate. HDI must name all 3 pillars with their measurement tool. T/F: "Nominal GDP does NOT measure wellbeing — neither does Real GDP" (both false for wellbeing). Target growth = 1.3% trend, not 4%+.
HIGH ≈ 20 pts
5
Economic/Business Cycle
Draw & label 4 phases (expansion, peak, recession/contraction, trough) Trend line Leading / Coincident / Lagging indicators (2 each) Growth vs. shrink headlines
Teacher focus: Graph must label all 4 phases AND the trend line. Leading indicators = signal future (incoming orders, construction permits). Coincident = happen simultaneously (unemployment numbers, import spending). Lagging = confirm after the fact (GDP, investments). Headlines: longer delivery times = growth (high demand).
HIGH ≈ 15 pts
6
Supply-Sided Fiscal Policy
Marginal income tax reduction → productivity chain Free trade benefits (social mobility, innovation) Laissez-faire vs. Intervention (T/F) Supply-sided measures before recession
Teacher focus: Must distinguish demand-sided vs. supply-sided — long-term production focus. Free trade: "naturally produces unequal results BUT creates social mobility and incentive to innovate." Laissez-faire = government should NOT intervene (free market maximizes welfare). Intervention = opposite.
HIGH ≈ 10 pts
7
Uneasy Quadrangle & External Equilibrium
4 elements of Uneasy Quadrangle 3 types of aim relationships (harmony/conflict/neutrality) Trade surplus/deficit, depreciation/appreciation effects Net exports calculation
Teacher focus: Elements: price stability, full employment, appropriate GDP growth, external equilibrium. Relationship examples must be specific (not vague). Depreciation = currency loses value → exports become cheaper for foreigners → exports increase. Trade surplus: not always desirable (foreign dependency risk).
MEDIUM ≈ 20 pts
8
Fair Distribution of Income (Lorenz Curve)
Label Lorenz curve chart axes Explain purpose of Lorenz curve Measures to flatten it (progressive tax, wealth redistribution) "Some people deserve to be poor" — argue/refute
Teacher focus: Axes not labelled = 0 pts for that part. Lorenz curve = x% of people own y% of income/wealth. Flattening measures must be substantive: progressive taxation + wealth/land confiscation. Opinion question: teacher rewards structured argumentation with a clear example.
MEDIUM ≈ 10 pts
Patterns

Key Insights from All 4 Exams

🔢
Calculation Tasks Are the Biggest Mark Sources
  • Pricing (cost-based), contribution margin, break-even, price elasticity, and real GDP calculations appear in every exam section
  • Showing formula first, then substituting, then final answer = full marks
  • Skipping the formula step typically costs 1 pt per calculation
  • Rounding must be reasonable (≈ symbol used correctly)
📊
Graphs Are High-Value but Highly Penalized
  • All graphs require: labelled axes, correct curve shapes, and correct phase/quadrant labels
  • PLC: missing "Development" phase (below x-axis) = lost pts
  • BCG: wrong product placement = automatic wrong answer
  • Break-even graph: must show 3 lines; break-even point must be circled/highlighted
  • Demand curve: axes must be inverted (price on x, demand on y)
💬
Application Examples Are Always Required
  • Every "explain" question expects a real-world example
  • Distribution strategies, pricing methods, fiscal policy all need examples
  • Vague answers like "competition" without context = partial marks only
  • Best examples: BYD (marketing), Red Bull (SWOT/PESTLE), PenPals (CM/BEP), Cinnamon Friends (pricing)
📋
True/False Sections Are Tricky — Know the Nuance
  • KA 3 had 16 T/F questions worth 16 BE — largest single task block
  • Key traps: "as little inflation as possible" = FALSE (target is ~2%, not zero)
  • "Usually" keyword in statements often changes T/F (short-run vs. long-run effects)
  • Deflation: short-run may increase demand, long-run causes decline
  • Laissez-faire = no intervention; Intervention = intervene. Both are opposite of each other
Teacher Analysis

Marking Behaviour — What the Teacher Rewards & Penalizes

Rewards (✓ marks in red)
  • Subject-specific terminology used correctly
  • Chain-of-logic explanations (cause → effect → consequence)
  • Full formula shown before substitution in calculations
  • Two distinct advantages AND disadvantages (not just one of each)
  • Explicitly linking theory to the given scenario/company
  • Noting abbreviations (CM1, CM2, PE) and defining them
  • "Well done" written for complete, clearly structured answers
Penalizes (✗ or half marks)
  • Vague answers without specifics ("it creates problems")
  • Missing axes labels on any graph
  • Incorrect classification in matrix tasks (BCG, Ansoff, Porter)
  • Confusing demand-pull and cost-push inflation
  • Misclassifying fixed vs. variable costs
  • Only naming a measure without explaining how it works
  • "Too vague" annotation for single-word threat/strength entries in SWOT
Expected Answer Structure
  • "Suggest" tasks: Name measure → Explain mechanism → Link to outcome
  • "Explain" tasks: Define term → Give example → State effect
  • "Calculate" tasks: Write formula → Substitute values → Calculate → State result in words
  • "Compare/Contrast": Two clear blocks, symmetric structure
  • "Analyse": Quantitative result + qualitative interpretation sentence
  • T/F: Just the letter. No explanation needed unless asked.
High-Scoring Answer Differentiators
  • Using the exact company from the scenario in examples (not generic firms)
  • Explaining WHY, not just WHAT — showing understanding of causality
  • Including the short-run vs. long-run distinction for economics topics
  • Connecting topics: e.g. price elasticity → demand curve → turnover calculation
  • Noting edge cases: e.g. "fuel cost questionable as fixed/variable"
  • Awareness of economic tradeoffs: surplus good but over-reliance risk
Study Guide

Answer Structure Templates

These exact patterns emerged from analyzing teacher corrections across all four exams. Memorize these structures.

📌 For "Suggest a measure" (Policy/Marketing)
[Name the measure] → "The government could lower income taxes."
[Mechanism] → "This gives people more disposable income to spend."
[Outcome chain] → "Higher demand → more production → more employment → GDP growth."
📌 For Break-Even Calculation
Q = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit)
→ Show formula → substitute → calculate → write: "At approx. X units, total costs are covered."
📌 For Price Elasticity
PE = (ΔQ/Q₁ × 100) ÷ (ΔP/P₁ × 100)
→ Always show formula first → substitute → result → "Since |PE| > 1, demand is elastic and sensitive to price changes."
📌 For Nominal vs. Real GDP
Nominal GDP: Total value of all goods/services at current prices. Does not account for inflation.
Real GDP: Adjusts for inflation using a base year. Better measure of actual productivity growth.
Formula: Real GDP = Nominal GDP ÷ (1 + inflation rate)
📌 For SWOT from a Text
Strength = internal positive (mentioned as current asset in text)
Weakness = internal negative (current limitation)
Opportunity = external positive (future possibility in text)
Threat = external negative (external risk mentioned)
→ Never write just one word — always 1 sentence minimum.
📌 For Recession Spiral (Question 5 type)
Demand ↓ (less income / higher costs) → Production ↓ (firms sell less, profits fall) → Employment ↓ (firms cut labour costs, lay-offs) → Income ↓ (loop accelerates downward)
Quick Reference

Must-Know Formulas & Definitions

Cost-Based Price
Total Cost per unit × (1 + profit margin %)
Relative Market Share
Company Turnover ÷ Strongest Competitor Turnover
CM I (per unit)
Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit
CM I (total)
CM I per unit × Quantity
CM II
CM I (total) − Related Fixed Costs
Break-Even Quantity
Fixed Costs ÷ CM I per unit
Real GDP
Nominal GDP ÷ (1 + inflation rate)
Turnover
Price × Demand (in pcs)
